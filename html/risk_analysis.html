Project Setup

Add details of the broad factors affecting this risk analysis.

Risk Categories

Add up to 6 risk categories covering the areas of risk for the project. For instance - Financial, Technical & Operational, Mangerial & Legal, Environmantal & Social, Health & Safety etc.


Risk Characteristics

Add up to 6 specific risks in each category, then define the risk as follows:

  • Likelihood of Occurrence - a percentage which reflects the probability that a risk factor may happen
  • Coefficient of Project Features - a percentage representing how the specifications, environment and unique nature of a project may help in mitigating risk impact
  • Controllability - a percentage which reflects the experience of the management team, their familiarity with the risk and the extent to which the team is able to mitigate its negative impact
  • Dependency - a percentage which is an adjustment factor for adjusting the impact of each individual risk after considering the co-existing risks
  • Risk weight - a percentage which reflects the overall importance of the risk. All risk weights for the category should sum up to 100

Grades of Impact

Assign 3 Impact Grades to aid assessment of risk to project over a range of scenarios. Impact Grades are a percantage of the initial cost of the project under analysis.

Degrees of Belief

Assign a Degree of Belief to each Impact Grade for each Risk. Degrees of Belief are percentages representing the expectation the the impact of this risk will reach the stated Impact Grade (the percentage of the initial project cost)

The row of Degrees of Belief for each Impact Grade should not exceed 100%, but can be lower (or zero)


Risk Assessment

This page provides a summary of risks aggregated by Category across the 3 areas of the project - Cost, Duration and Quality

  • Aggregated Risk - the aggregation of all risks in this category based on the Risk Characteristics and Impact Assessment data entry
  • Unassigned Degrees of Belief - where there is a lack of information in the Impact Assessment (where rows on Degrees of Belief table do not total 100%) this represents uncertainty about that aspect of the project, which is calculated as a percentage representing 'Ignorance' in this area
  • Objective Weights - by default each area (project cost, duration and quality) is assumed to have an equal level - these weightings can be altered if required
  • Assessment of Risks - the aggregation of all areas of impact in this category - this represents the categorys potential impact on the project as a whole, with associated level of ingorance
  • Risk Range - when the category ingorance is assigned, the range of risk impact can be calculated with a minimum, maximum and the average risk of impact
  • Potential cost impact on project - represents the potential financial impact on project based on the Average risk level

Assessment aggregation on a Project Objective Level

This page provides an aggregation of risk levels of categories over the whole project

  • Category Weights - each category can have a weight assigned to it adjusting its importance in relation to other categories - defualt assumption is all categories are eqaully weighted - All category weights should sum up to 100

Aggregation risk assessment results on the project as a whole

This page provides the results of risk levels for the project as a whole

  • Project Weights - each area can have a weight assigned to it adjusting its importance in relation to other areas - defualt assumption is all areas are eqaully weighted - All area weights should sum up to 100

Operating Guidance

Risk Model:

In this tool, risk is modelled as :    R = L * P * I

Likelihood of occurrence: represents the probability of an identified risk factor to happen.

Impact: represents the maximum amount of loss or damage incurred on a specific project success objective if the risk factor happened. In this research, the impact is assessed as a percentage of the project initial cost.

Probability of Impact materialisation: reflects the probability that the actual impact of a risk on a project success objective will materialise in its worst scenario.

Probability of impact materialisation play the role of adjustment factor which assist the analyst in discounting the maximum potential risk impact by considering the surrounding environment.

As illustrated by the aforementioned risk model, risk assessment is related to the interdependencies between the analysed risk and the other identified risks, the project specifications and its environment, the capability and the experience of the project management team who is in charge of managing risk impact. These issues have been considered through the usage of three adjustment coefficient; Project Features (F), Controllability of risk impact (C) and risk Dependency (D) on the other identified risks.

Project Features (F):

This factor reflects the specifications, environment, criticality and the unique nature of a construction project. The value of this coefficient is to be determined by the decision maker or the risk analyst. It is proposed by the author that its value is a proportional score falls in the range [0-1].

The risk analyst, with the assistance of his experience and the expert opinions, is required to provide an agreed upon value for this factor which reflect his/her belief about the mitigating effect of the project features on the maximum risk impact. If the risk analyst believes, for instance, that the project environment has a very supportive role which may mitigate the maximum impact, he/she is to assign a value for the features parameter which reflects such effect. For instance the value of F may be assigned as 0.6. However, if it is the opposite case, the value of F may be assigned as 0.95 or even 1 to show that there is no positive effect the project environment has in mitigating risk impact.

Controllability (C):

Reflects the experience of the project management team, their familiarity with the risk and the extent to which the team is able to manage it and mitigate its downside impact. Controllability can also be looked at as a means of reflecting the nature of a risk factor as some risks are more manageable than others. Hence, it is a reflection of risk nature and a measurement of the experience, familiarity and capability of the party in charge of managing it and mitigating its negative impact on a specific project target. Similar to the previous two factors, controllability is an adjustment coefficient whose value falls in the range [0-1].

Dependency Factor (D):

Assuming that total risk impact is the sum of the impacts of individual risks is not realistic as it assumes total independence between the identified risk factors. This is an exaggerating estimate which may produce an over-estimated project risk level. To appreciate this issue, the author is introducing the Dependency coefficient as an adjustment factor to be used for discounting the impact of each individual risk after considering the co-existing risks. The rational behind this introduction is that the identified risks will create a Risk Environment affecting the project. Hence the collective behaviour of the risk factors are not to be neglected.

The values of the three coefficients F, C and D will be used to produce a value for the parameter P; the probability of impact materialisation. We can generate the value of P in different ways. Based on the above definitions of F,C and D, we can use the weighted average method. We can assume that the three coefficients are equally important which will make P the average of them. The risk analyst, however, can make different assumption based on his attitude towards the risk under analysis.

Achievability coefficient (A):

Achievability coefficient (A) is an adjustment factor which reflects the degree to which the analyst believes that the benefit under analysis will materialise in its estimated size. This coefficient will reflect the ability of the project team of delivering the project benefits in their expected volume during the bidding stage. It will also reflect whether there is a lack of clarity and details of the project brief which makes the total benefit of the project outcome is hardly be estimated.
